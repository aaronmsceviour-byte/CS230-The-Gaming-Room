# CS230-The-Gaming-Room

## Who was the client and what did they want?
The client was The Gaming Room. They asked for a web-based version of their game Draw It or Lose It. The game should run 4 1-minute rounds where teams will guess as the pictures are slowly revealed. The core requirments were to have only one instance of the game running at a time, all games and team names need to be unique within the system, and the application must work across all major platforms and devices.

## What did I do well?
I feel two sections stand out. The storage managment section went into techical depth, breaking down various types of allocation. I also feel that my evaluation and comparison section is worth noting. By woeking through the pros and cons of Linux, Windoes, Mac, and Mobile I was able to deduce the best development environments for this project. Additionally, the experience has led me into thinking of running a system with Linux as a side project to learn more about it.

## What about the design document was helpful when developing the code?
Seeing all the work laid
out in one place gave me a deeper understanding of what we were actually building. It was easier to come back and reflect on decisions already made and see how they connected. It also helped reinforce that development is not a one and done process. It is slow and methodical, and the document is something you return to and refine as the project evolves to keep the work aligned with the original goals.

## What would I revise?
The executive summary could be stronger. It covers the basics but reads more like a compressed list than a true standalone overview. A stronger summary would give any stakeholder a complete picture of the project before they read anything else. In future projects I would invest more time making that section truly self-contained. I would also complete the requirements section even when it is not being assessed. Having that fully documented gives a stronger foundation for every design decision that follows.

## How did I interpret the user's needs and implement them into the design?
I worked through the client interview and noted every mention of a desired feature, system function, or requirement. When multiple stakeholders pointed to the same need I marked it as a higher priority. Consensus across multiple people signals something more essential than a single request. Those high priority items directly shaped the core design decisions in the document.

## How did I approach designing software and what would I do again?
Starting with the interview and spending real time analyzing the requirements before touching any code gave me a much bigger picture of the system upfront. From there using UML diagrams to map out class relationships was crucial. Visualizing the system made the structure ten times easier to understand and kept the code intentional rather than improvised. Letting those requirements directly drive design pattern choices, like choosing the Singleton specifically because the client needed one game instance at a time, is something I will carry into every future project.
